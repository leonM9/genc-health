Chapter 1 · Background of the Study

The Local Crisis

Why Filipino medical records need a new foundation.

Two converging failures expose the limits of centralized legacy infrastructure in the Philippine healthcare and civil-registry sectors.

Confidentiality Failure

PhilHealth Medusa Attack (2023)

Exposed millions of citizen records due to absence of encryption-at-rest and a single-point-of-failure database (former DICT Sec. Uy, 2023).

Integrity Failure

Civil-Registry Forgery

The Alice Guo case revealed how the Late Registration system was exploited to backdate authentic-looking PSA documents — undetectable under manual verification (Sen. Gatchalian, 2024).

Gen C addresses both failures simultaneously by combining a hybrid encryption layer with the Layered Proof Aggregation (LPA) protocol.

How can a decentralized architecture prevent mass data exfiltration in the event of a breach?
2
How can immutable timestamping prevent retroactive document falsification, as observed in fraudulent late registrations?

Chapter 1 · Objectives of the Study
04 / 13
Section 1.3

One general goal. Five measurable commitments.

General Objective

To design and develop a decentralized web application that uses AES-256, CP-ABE, and the Layered Proof Aggregation (LPA) protocol to provide a scalable, cost-effective, and privacy-preserving mechanism for verifying hospital records under a hybrid blockchain architecture.

  • SO 1. Build three integrated modules — a Registry Portal, a Patient Wallet, and a Provider Portal.
  • SO 2. Implement privacy-preserving verification through Zero-Knowledge Proofs (proof without disclosure).
  • SO 3. Deploy the proprietary LPA protocol to achieve ≥ 280 TPS at < $0.01 per record.
  • SO 4. Integrate AES-256 and CP-ABE to secure off-chain payloads with granular access control.
  • SO 5. Evaluate software quality against ISO/IEC 25010 through structured UAT with hospital staff.
Chapter 1 · Scope & Delimitations
05 / 13
Section 1.7

Where the study begins — and where it deliberately stops.

Scope

What the study covers

• Three modules: Admin Dashboard, Hospital Interface, Patient Wallet
• Dual-chain architecture: private permissioned testnet for consensus
• AES-256 payload encryption + CP-ABE access policy
• IPFS for off-chain storage of heavy documents
• LPA protocol tested for throughput and gas reduction
• Evaluated under ISO/IEC 25010 (functional suitability, performance, security)

Delimitations

What lies outside this study

• No native mobile app (iOS / Android); web-based for desktop & tablet only
• No offline-first capability for GIDA areas without connectivity
• Synthetic data only (10,000 dummy records via Python) — strict RA 10173 compliance
• No OCR / ETL of legacy paper records held by the PSA
• No biometric hardware (fingerprint / iris); cryptographic identity only

These delimitations preserve the integrity of the experimental design while keeping the study within the proponents' resource envelope.

Chapter 3 · Methodology & System Architecture
08 / 13
Sections 3.1 – 3.3

Design Science Research, executed in three layers.

The proponents adopt Design Science Research Methodology (DSRM) — iterative cycles of problem identification, artefact design, construction, demonstration, and rigorous evaluation.

Layer 1 · Presentation

React.js + Wasm

Responsive frontend with client-side AES-256-GCM encryption — cleartext medical data never leaves the user's device.

Layer 2 · Logic

Node.js Middleware

Hosts the CP-ABE authority for attribute-based key issuance and the LPA service that batches records into Merkle roots.

Layer 3 · Data

EVM Permissioned Ledger + IPFS

Encrypted payloads pinned to a private IPFS cluster; only the Merkle root is anchored on-chain via QBFT consensus.

Authentication uses SMTP-OTP multi-factor only — SMS is rejected to defeat SIM-swap attacks and to remain accessible across GIDA satellite links.
Chapter 3 · Algorithmic Core
09 / 13
Sections 3.5.1 – 3.5.6

Three algorithms. Three orthogonal jobs.

Algorithm I · Confidentiality

AES-256-GCM

Symmetric cipher with 256-bit key — encrypts the medical payload (PDFs, DICOMs, abstracts) before it leaves the browser. Chosen over RSA-2048 for throughput on multi-megabyte files.

Algorithm II · Access Control

CP-ABE

Ciphertext-Policy Attribute-Based Encryption. The AES key is encapsulated under a policy expression (e.g., role=Cardiologist ∧ status=On-Duty) — only attribute holders can decrypt.

Algorithm III · Scalability

Layered Proof Aggregation

Merkle-tree batching of N record hashes into a single root. One on-chain anchor settles the entire batch, collapsing complexity from O(n) to O(log n).

Chapter 3 · Evaluation & Testing Methods
10 / 13
Sections 3.6 – 3.8

Four testing tiers, anchored on ISO/IEC 25010.

Tier 1 · Unit Testing

Cryptographic primitives

AES-256, CP-ABE, hashing, and signature verification — automated coverage in CI pipelines.

Tier 2 · Integration Testing

LPA, IPFS, Anchor Flow

End-to-end batch issuance and Merkle anchoring — gas measured via hardhat-gas-reporter; latency via custom JS scripts.

Tier 3 · Security Testing

Adversarial Red-Team

Simulated data leaks, key-management compromise, smart-contract injection, and privilege escalation.

Tier 4 · User Acceptance

ISO/IEC 25010 Survey

5-point Likert scale across functional suitability, performance, usability, reliability, and security — administered to 10 purposively-sampled IT and clinical respondents.

Testing input: 10,000 synthetic records generated via Python — zero exposure of real patient data, in full compliance with RA 10173.

Chapters 5 & 6 · Conclusion and Recommendations
12 / 13
Sections 5.2 & 6.1

A capable, effective, and standards-aligned artefact.

Conclusion

The proponents conclude that Gen C is a capable and effective decentralized solution that meets ISO/IEC 25010 standards across functional stability, security, usability, and reliability. The system successfully integrates blockchain anchoring with hybrid encryption to address the vulnerabilities of centralized medical-record management — reinforcing its potential for real-world deployment in the Philippine healthcare sector.

  • R1. Integrate Gen C with existing EHR systems used in local hospitals.
  • R2. Harden authentication with biometric or FIDO2 hardware keys.
  • R3. Expand to dedicated iOS / Android mobile clients.
  • R4. Use IPFS for high-resolution X-ray, MRI, and laboratory imaging.
  • R5. Conduct longitudinal trust studies on self-custody of encryption keys.
  • R6. Implement continuous, automated smart-contract auditing.
  • R7. Partner with the DOH and National Privacy Commission for compliance.
  • R8. Add multi-modal explanations for non-technical end-users.
